Sprint Goal

Summary

The Sprint Goal communicates why a Sprint is valuable to stakeholders. Although the Sprint Goal is a commitment by the Developers, it provides flexibility in terms of the exact work needed to achieve it. The Sprint Goal also creates coherence and focus, encouraging the Scrum Team to work together rather than on separate initiatives.

  • The Sprint goal should be valuable and you should focus on the “why”. Why are you doing this Sprint? What is better than the Sprint before? Why should the customer pay for it?

  • Therefore, the Sprint goal should be telling. Do not describe it too technical. Non-technical stakeholders should understand the value of the Sprint. Avoid goals that basically mean “finish ticket X, Y and Z”.

  • The Sprint Goal has to be measurable: After a Sprint, it must be very clear whether the Goal was achieved or not. This is binary. No “nearly achieved”, etc.

  • Show the achieved goal in the Review. If possible, a demo is preferable to showing code or tickets. Be creative here. Customer love when something flashes or moves .

Further Explanation

The Sprint Goal communicates why a Sprint is valuable to stakeholders. Although the Sprint Goal is a commitment by the Developers, it provides flexibility in terms of the exact work needed to achieve it. The Sprint Goal also creates coherence and focus, encouraging the Scrum Team to work together rather than on separate initiatives.

The Sprint Goal is created during the Sprint Planning event and then added to the Sprint Backlog. As the Developers work during the Sprint, they keep the Sprint Goal in mind. If the work turns out to be different than they expected, they collaborate with the Product Owner to negotiate the scope of the Sprint Backlog within the Sprint without affecting the Sprint Goal.

Source: Scrum Guide

Choose goals that cover all domains!

However: In the perfect Scrum world, there is one Sprint Goal and the complete (cross-functional) team can work on that one goal. At grandcentrix, we are not always in that perfect world because we serve a broad range of domains (hardware, firmware, backend, frontend, mobile apps) and, of course, not everyone can do every kind of work.

  • If there is no reasonable cross-domain goal, it is okay to have more than one goal.

  • Having “End-2-End” goals furthermore decrease risk (do the parts work together?) and are much more vivid for the customer.

Avoid creating as many goals as there are tasks (“finish the backlog!”)

So, it is okay if you move some tickets into the next Sprint as long as the Sprint Goal is met. (In a (not 100% cross-functional) team, finishing exactly all tickets would mean that some people run idle before others are done with ‘their’ task).

Use Sprint Goals as smaller Steps to a Product Goal and a Product Vision

Many times when we engage with a client to provide them with a solution or a product, they will have a certain vision (no matter how vague it might be) about what they would like to achieve or reach with their product or solution (the “why”).

Therefore, providing us with a bigger picture and general conditions for the product or solution we as a team are working on. Oftentimes however, clients don't know how to reach their vision or goal. (Hint: That’s what we are there for. ).

To support our clients in finding the appropriate technical solution to their vision (the “how”) the way to go is brake down the Vision a client has into separate Product Goals, which are then reached within several sprints. The Sprint Goal is the tool which explains to the team, how the sprint is contributing to reaching the current product goal.

Try to establish a “last Sprint mentality”

  • Try to think that the current Sprint (that delivers the next increment) is the last one ever in the project.

  • If gcx would stop working after the next Review, the customer would still have product that is working and that delivers some value

  • Of course, the scope of this “product” might be limited

  • But thinking this way stops the team from accumulating to much technical debt, ensures that the product is always in a functional state, and pays value to the fact that projects do not commercially run forever but that the customer expects to be done with the project at some point in the future.

A common misunderstanding:

Not all work that is done within a Sprint has to be covered by the Sprint Goal.

Depending on your project, the work on the goal(s) might represent something like 40-90% of the complete team capacity. There is room for others tasks as well.

However, if attaining the goal is in danger, the team should focus on the tasks that pay on the goal.
