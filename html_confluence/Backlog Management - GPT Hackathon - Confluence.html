Summary

Our target picture of a product backlog is an emergent, ordered and prioritized task list of everything that needs to be done within a project to build and improve the product. It is the single source for the work done by the project team and ideally thus also directly forms the basis for the roadmap.
Consequently, this means: If a job isn’t listed on the backlog, then it doesn’t get done!

The agile product backlog is a prioritized list of deliverables that need to be implemented through the development process. There are many factors that determine how backlog items get prioritized in an agile project backlog. These can include the importance of the item for the end-user and development difficulty. 

At grandcentrix, the product backlog…

  • …is an ordered list of everything that is known to be needed in the product.

  • …is never complete, it’s a living artifact.

  • …lists all features, functions, requirements, enhancements and fixes to be made to the product in future releases.

  • …changes permanently caused by changes in business requirements, market conditions or technology etc.

Further Explanation

The product owner is in charge of the backlog, including its content and order. But — and this is important — unlike traditional team schedules or to-do lists, the product owner isn’t in charge of defining when each individual task is to be completed. That’s down to the team members. A product backlog makes both developer’s and product owner’s lives easier; instead of the dev team working at someone else’s pace and having work pushed on them, they can instead look at the product backlog, work out which jobs need to be done in what order, and pull jobs from it when they’re ready. This means they can focus on doing the best job they can, free from the stress of unrealistic deadlines and frequent interruptions.

Because everything is transparent, product owners and other team members alike are always up to date on work. This greater level of openness helps with iteration planning, as well as budgeting and expectation management.

How can good quality backlog management be achieved?

DEEP is a useful concept to be applied in the Product Backlog refinement process which involves the act of adding detail, estimates, and order to items in the Product Backlog and keeping it in shape. Product Backlog items have the attributes of a description (Detailed appropriately), Story Points (Estimated), order (Prioritized) and they are continuously to be added, removed and updated (Emergent) in the backlog to reflected to understanding of the backlog of the team in just-in-time and just-enough manner.

  • Detailed Appropriately - The stories at the top of the product backlog will be worked on in the next sprint. Therefore, these items must be fully defined so that the team can work on them without obstacles. 

  • Estimated - The items are estimated. The items at the top of the backlog have accurate estimates. All other items are estimated at high level and will be re-estimated as the team gets more information.

  • Emergent - Product Backlog is not static, it is in constant change. As the project progresses, more and more information and knowledge are obtained, and the user stories in the product Backlog are also added, removed, or rearranged.

  • Prioritized - Prioritized means that each element (as long as it is not the first or last) always has one element that is more important and one that is less important. So always a predecessor and successor.

In the grandcentrix solution project reality, the initial product backlog at the beginning of the project must already reflect the entire range of functions expected by customers and stakeholders.

How to prioritize a backlog?

Why does it have to be?
In an agile setup, we always expect unforeseen things. So it can happen that we run out of money in the project, more important things than our project are suddenly pushed forward or the like. If we always know exactly what the most important element is and we don't get lost in negative multitasking, then we have a very good chance that the important things will also be completed.

Requirements for a valid backlog

For a backlog to be resilient, its components - aka Product Backlog Items (PBI) - must be load-bearing.

Product Backlog Items can range from specifications and requirements to use cases, epics, user stories, even bugs or time-limited research tasks. A PBI must have at least the following characteristics:

  • Self-explanatory title

  • Brief management summary in "prose" (What is the goal of the PBI?)

  • Acceptance criteria / test scenario

  • Estimate: The team must estimate the relative effort required to complete the PBI (see definition of done).

  • Priority: the product owner must prioritize the PBIs according to their relative value.
